Detailed Hinglish Explanation (Para by Para)

1. Setting the Scene (Hope for Rain)

"The house—the only one in the entire valley—sat on the crest of a low hill. From this height one could see the river and the field of ripe corn dotted with the flowers that always promised a good harvest."

Hinglish Analysis: Yeh story shuru hoti hai Lencho ke ghar se. Uska ghar poori valley (ghati) mein akela tha, ek choti si pahadi ke top (crest) par. Wahan se Lencho aasani se nadi (river) aur apne ripe corn (pakki hui makke ki kheti) ko dekh sakta tha. Corn ke field mein flowers (phool) the, jo yeh promise (vaada) karte the ki is saal bahut achhi fasal hogi. Isse pata chalta hai ki Lencho ka ghar kitna isolated (alag-thlag) aur kitna beautiful (sundar) tha, aur woh apni fasal par kitna confident (vishwas se bhara) tha.

Word Meanings:

  • Valley: Ghati (A low area between hills or mountains).
  • Crest: Choti / Shikhar (The top of a hill).
  • Ripe: Pakka hua (Fully developed and ready to be gathered or eaten).
  • Dotted: Bichhe hue (Scattered or interspersed with).
  • Harvest: Fasal / Katai (The process of gathering crops).

2. The Disaster (Hailstorm Destruction)

"Not a leaf remained on the trees. The corn was totally destroyed. The flowers were gone from the plants. Lencho's soul was filled with sadness."

Hinglish Analysis: Phir achanak heavy hailstorm (bade-bade ole) shuru ho gaye, jisne sab kuch destroy (barbaad) kar diya. Pedon par ek bhi patta (leaf) nahi bacha aur poori fasal khatam ho gayi. Lencho ka dil (soul) bahut dukh (sadness) se bhar gaya. Use laga jaise ek plague of locusts (tiddiyon ka jhund) bhi itni barbaadi nahi karta. Ab uske paas aur uske parivar ke paas khane ke liye kuch nahi tha.

Word Meanings:

  • Hailstorm: Ole (Shower of pellets of ice).
  • Destroyed: Barbaad ho gaya (Wrecked).
  • Plague: Musibat / Sankat (A large number of harmful animals, like locusts).
  • Soul: Aatma / Dil (Spiritual part of a person).

3. The Solution (Faith in God)

"But in the hearts of all who lived in that solitary house in the middle of the valley, there was a single hope: help from God. Don't be so upset, even though this seems like a total loss. Remember, no one dies of hunger."

Hinglish Analysis: Jab Lencho ki hope (umeed) poori tarah se toot chuki thi, tab bhi uske dil (hearts) mein ek single hope (ek hi umeed) bachi thi: Bhagwan se madad (help from God). Woh bahut religious (dharmik) tha aur usko firm belief (pakka vishwas) tha ki Bhagwan kabhi kisi ko bhukha nahi marne dete (no one dies of hunger). Use yaad aaya ki Bhagwan ki aankhein sab dekhti hain, chahe woh kitna bhi deep (conscience) ho. Isliye usne Bhagwan ko 100 pesos ke liye letter likhne ka decision (nirnay) liya.

Word Meanings:

  • Solitary: Akela (Existing alone).
  • Conscience: Zameer / Andruni gyaan (An inner sense of right and wrong).
  • Deep: Gehra (Profound).
  • Hope: Umeed (A feeling of expectation and desire).

4. The Postmaster's Initiative (Kindness)

"The Postmaster—a fat, amiable fellow—also broke out laughing, but almost immediately he turned serious and, tapping the letter on his desk, commented, 'What faith! I wish I had the faith of the man who wrote this letter.'"

Hinglish Analysis: Postmaster, jo ki ek mota aur friendly (amiable) aadmi tha, pehle toh zor se hasa (broke out laughing) jab usne dekha ki letter par address 'To God' likha hai. Par agle hi pal woh serious (gambhir) ho gaya. Usne letter ko desk par patakte (tapping) hue kaha, "Kya vishwas (faith) hai! Kaash, mujh mein bhi us aadmi jaisa bharosa hota." Usne decide kiya ki woh us farmer ki faith ko break (todna) nahi chahta. Isliye usne apne employees aur doston se paise collect (ikhattha) kiye aur khud bhi salary ka part diya, par woh sirf 70 pesos hi ikhattha kar paya.

Word Meanings:

  • Amiable: Friendly / Dayaalu (Having or displaying a friendly and pleasant manner).
  • Tapping: Patakna / Halka thapthapana (Strike lightly and quickly).
  • Commented: Tippani ki (Expressed an opinion).
  • Faith: Vishwas / Bharosa (Complete trust or confidence).

5. The Great Irony (Ingratitude)

"He showed not the slightest surprise on seeing the money; such was his confidence... But when he counted the money, he became angry. God could not have made a mistake, nor could He have denied Lencho what he had requested."

Hinglish Analysis: Jab Lencho ne agley Sunday aakar money receive (paise liye), toh usne zara bhi surprise (not the slightest surprise) nahi dikhaya. Usko apne confidence (vishwas) par itna bharosa tha. Lekin jaise hi usne paise giney (counted the money), woh ghussa (angry) ho gaya. Use lagta tha ki Bhagwan aisi galti (mistake) nahi kar sakte aur na hi uski request (maang) ko mana (denied) kar sakte hain. Usne turant Post Office employees par chori karne ka ilzaam lagaya aur baaki ke paise maangte hue doosra letter likha, jisme unhe "bunch of crooks" (beimaan logon ka jhund) kaha.

Word Meanings:

  • Slightest: Zara sa (Very small).
  • Confidence: Vishwas (The state of feeling certain about the truth of something).
  • Counted: Gina (To determine the total number).
  • Denied: Mana kiya (Refused to give).
  • Crooks: Beimaan / Dhokebaaz (Dishonest people).

The Story Unfolds: A Narrative Review (Creative Summary)

A tale of pure faith, cruel nature, and bitter irony.

Meet Lencho, a simple farmer whose lone house sat atop a low hill, his fields bursting with the promise of a golden corn harvest. He needed only one thing: a good downpour. When the rain finally arrived, it brought not blessings, but a disaster in disguise—an hour-long hailstorm. The world turned white, and within minutes, his entire crop was stripped away, leaving the trees barren and Lencho's soul filled with an unbearable sadness.

But where man fails, Lencho knew God never would. Driven by a child-like, unshakeable faith, he decided to become one of the most unique correspondents in history: he wrote a letter to God, requesting one hundred pesos to save his family from starvation.

At the post office, the cheerful and kindly Postmaster read the divine address and burst into laughter, but his heart was instantly moved by such profound conviction. He chose not to break that faith. Gathering money from his colleagues and contributing himself, he managed to collect 70 pesos. He sealed the money in an envelope and signed it: 'God'.

The final act is a study in irony. Lencho receives the money with zero surprise, his faith demanding the reply. Yet, upon counting it, his confidence turns to rage. He instantly writes a second letter to God, not thanking him, but complaining that the post office staff must be thieves. The very people who acted out of selfless kindness were branded a "bunch of crooks" by the man they sacrificed for. This simple story powerfully questions whether human charity can ever match the demands of absolute faith.

Character Sketch: Lencho

1. Hardworking Farmer:

Lencho was an 'ox of a man' (very strong and hardworking), who knew his fields intimately. He worked tirelessly, hoping for a good crop, demonstrating his dedication to his work and family.

2. Unwavering Faith:

His faith in God was absolute, almost childish. He was certain that God would help him. This deep belief made him write a letter asking for a specific sum, which is the central theme of the story.

3. Simple and Naive:

He was naive (simple-minded) about the outside world. He knew how to write a letter, but didn't question how God would send the money. His simplicity is what makes his faith so pure yet his subsequent actions so ironic.

4. Ungrateful and Ironic:

This is his biggest flaw. When he received the 70 pesos, he showed no gratitude. Instead, he instantly jumped to the conclusion that the Post Office employees, who had just helped him, were thieves (crooks). This irony is the whole point of the story: his pure faith in God makes him mistrustful of fellow men.

Important Questions: 30 Questions Bank (SAQs, LAQs & PYQs)

Short Answer Questions (SAQ - 10 Questions)

Q1: What did Lencho hope for? Why did he say the raindrops were like new coins?

A: Lencho, a farmer, hoped for a good downpour to ensure a successful harvest. He compared the raindrops to 'new coins' because they were crucial for his crop's survival. The drops symbolized the economic value the rain held for his family's livelihood.

Q2: Describe Lencho's house and its location. What was the significance of its location?

A: The house was the only one in the entire valley and sat on the crest of a low hill. Its location was significant because from that height, he could easily view his fields, and later, it became the only place from which he could gaze at the ruined land and wait for divine help.

Q3: What was Lencho's only hope after the hailstorm?

A: After the hailstorm destroyed everything, Lencho had only one hope left: help from God. He believed firmly that God's eyes see everything, even what is deep in one's conscience, and would not let anyone die of hunger.

Q4: Why did Lencho become angry when he received the money?

A: Lencho became angry because he had requested 100 pesos but received only 70 pesos. His absolute faith made him believe that God could not make a mistake or deny him his request, leading him to conclude that the post office employees had stolen the missing amount.

Q5: Who or what did Lencho have complete faith in? What did he do on the following Sunday?

A: Lencho had complete faith in God. On the following Sunday, he went to the post office even earlier than usual to inquire if there was a letter for him, showing his firm confidence in a divine response.

Q6: Why did the Postmaster laugh at the beginning? When did he become serious?

A: The Postmaster laughed when he saw the address 'To God' on the letter, as it was absurd. He became serious almost immediately when he recognized the depth of the sender's faith, wishing he possessed the same conviction.

Q7: What did the Postmaster need to fulfill Lencho's request? How much was he able to collect?

A: Lencho had requested 100 pesos. The Postmaster was unable to collect the full amount, despite contributing part of his own salary and asking for contributions from his employees and friends. He was only able to collect a total of 70 pesos.

Q8: What did Lencho call the post office employees in his second letter, and why?

A: Lencho called the post office employees a "bunch of crooks" (beimaan log) in his second letter. He believed they had stolen the remaining 30 pesos that God must have surely sent, completely unaware that these very people had tried to help him.

Q9: What is the main conflict of the story?

A: The story highlights two conflicts: Man vs. Nature (Lencho vs. the devastating hailstorm) and the main Man vs. Man (The ironic conflict between Lencho's faith and his mistrust of the Post Office employees).

Q10: What steps did Lencho take immediately after his crop was destroyed?

A: Lencho spent a whole night pondering over the disaster. Knowing that "no one dies of hunger" because of God, he decided to write a letter to God, asking for financial help to sow his field again.

Long Answer Questions (LAQ/PYQ - 20 Questions)

Q11: How does the story "A Letter to God" focus on the conflict between man and man? (PYQ)

A: The story highlights the conflict between the Postmaster's charity and Lencho's ingratitude. The Postmaster and his staff represent human kindness by sacrificing their own money to help a stranger. Lencho, however, fueled by his absolute but narrow faith, suspects them of theft. This ironic accusation of the "bunch of crooks" reveals a tragic conflict where a man's faith in God makes him mistrust his fellow human beings.

Q12: 'Faith can move mountains.' Do you agree with this statement in the context of the story? (Thematic Question)

A: Yes, it is true in a figurative sense. Lencho's powerful faith in God compelled the Postmaster to gather resources and respond to the letter. His faith successfully "moved the mountain" of human indifference, inspiring an act of charity. However, the story subtly cautions that faith, when extreme, can sometimes blind a person to the goodness of man.

Q13: Give a character sketch of the Postmaster. How did his actions reflect his noble nature?

A: The Postmaster was a fat, amiable (friendly) and sympathetic man. His actions show his nobility: he was first amused, but quickly became serious and deeply moved by Lencho's faith. He decided to help not out of duty, but to preserve Lencho's belief. He acted as an agent of God, asking for contributions and giving part of his salary, reflecting his kindness, empathy, and humanist spirit.

Q14: Do you think Lencho was justified in calling the post office employees a 'bunch of crooks'? Justify your answer.

A: No, Lencho was absolutely not justified. The employees were selfless benefactors who pooled their own money to help him. His accusation was based purely on his naivety and unshakeable belief that God would send exactly 100 pesos. He was too simple-minded to consider that humans had intervened, making him ungrateful and ironically cruel to his saviours.

Q15: Lencho was a devout man. Elaborate on his devotion and how it proved ironic.

A: Lencho's devotion was absolute; he believed God's eyes saw into his soul and would answer his need, prompting him to write a literal letter to God. The irony is that his powerful faith in a divine being made him mistrustful of the only human beings who demonstrated selfless charity. His inability to comprehend that God works through people led him to condemn his helpers as thieves.

Q16: How did the hailstorm affect Lencho's field? What were his feelings afterward?

A: The hailstorm was catastrophic. It lasted for an hour, covering the valley in a blanket of white, like it was covered in salt. The corn was totally destroyed, and not a single leaf remained on the trees. Lencho's soul was filled with profound sadness. He felt the loss was worse than a plague of locusts, fearing they would starve the entire year.

Q17: How did the Postmaster manage to save Lencho's faith in God?

A: The Postmaster saved Lencho's faith by deciding to answer the letter. He gathered 70 pesos, put it in an envelope, and, to maintain the illusion of divine intervention, signed the letter simply as 'God'. This action, requiring sacrifice and coordination from the employees, successfully reinforced Lencho's conviction.

Q18: Contrast Lencho's reaction on receiving the letter with the Postmaster's satisfaction. What does this contrast highlight?

A: Lencho showed not the slightest surprise; his confidence was so great he expected the money. The Postmaster, however, felt the 'contentment of a man who has performed a good deed'. This contrast highlights the central irony: the giver finds joy in his charity, while the receiver reacts with rage, showcasing the difference between human goodness and blind faith.

Q19: What moral or message does the story convey regarding faith and humanity?

A: The story conveys that while unwavering faith is powerful, it can sometimes be narrow and self-centered. It shows the presence of profound human goodness (the Postmaster's charity) but simultaneously criticizes the human tendency to mistrust others (Lencho's accusation) when things don't go exactly as expected. The true message is the complexity of human interaction versus divine belief.

Q20: Describe the appearance and behavior of the hailstones. How long did the storm last?

A: The hailstones were large, compared to new silver coins. The storm was so intense that the ground was covered in white, resembling a field of salt. The storm lasted for one whole hour, long enough to completely ruin the corn crop and leave Lencho's family destitute.

Q21: Why did the author use the ironic ending where Lencho calls his helpers 'crooks'?

A: The author, G.L. Fuentes, used this ironic ending to stress the central theme of blind faith vs. human compassion. It creates a powerful, unforgettable climax that leaves the reader questioning who the "crooks" truly are: the generous post office staff, or a nature so cruel, or perhaps the limitations of Lencho's faith itself.

Q22: How would you describe Lencho's relationship with God based on the way he communicated?

A: Lencho's relationship with God was direct, transactional, and demanding. He viewed God as a specific provider who would respond to a letter with exact financial assistance. His communication was not of a desperate, pleading devotee, but of a man making a valid claim for assistance, showing immense confidence and simplicity.

Q23: What is the significance of the title "A Letter to God"?

A: The title is significant because it encapsulates the central plot device and the main theme. The physical letter represents Lencho's immense faith and the bridge between the man's suffering and his belief in divine power. The entire story revolves around the composition, mailing, and response to this extraordinary letter.

Q24: Discuss the theme of irony as depicted in the story.

A: The story is rich in irony. Situational Irony occurs when the Postmaster and his staff, who intended to fulfill the role of God by an act of charity, are rewarded with an accusation of theft. Lencho's faith, which saved him, ironically causes him to condemn his saviors. This contrast between expectation and reality defines the story.

Q25: What does the phrase "ox of a man" imply about Lencho, and how does it relate to the story's theme?

A: The phrase "ox of a man" implies that Lencho was extremely strong, hardworking, and laborious, dedicating himself fully to his fields. This relates to the theme of Man vs. Nature: despite his brute strength and tireless labor, he is utterly helpless against the destructive power of the hailstorm, showing nature's ultimate cruelty.

Q26: What did Lencho write in his second letter to God? What does this tell you about his character?

A: In his second letter, Lencho asked God to send the remaining 30 pesos, but told him not to send it through the mail because the post office employees were a "bunch of crooks." This shows his simplicity, blind faith in God's infallibility, and severe mistrust of human beings.

Q27: Contrast Lencho the farmer and Lencho the man of faith.

A: Lencho the farmer is pragmatic, hardworking, and understands the rhythm of nature (predicting rain). Lencho the man of faith is naive, deeply religious, and simplistic, believing that God operates like a post office. The conflict arises when the farmer's world is destroyed, forcing the man of faith to act, leading to the central irony.

Q28: How did Lencho's intense belief make him incapable of believing in human goodness?

A: Lencho's belief was so intense that he believed God was the only source of help. Since God cannot make a mistake, the shortfall of 30 pesos could only mean one thing: theft by the human intermediaries. This intense, narrow focus on divine perfection made him completely blind to the tangible, selfless goodness shown by the post office staff.

Q29: Why did the Postmaster decide to sign the letter as 'God'?

A: The Postmaster signed the letter as 'God' to create the perfect illusion and ensure that Lencho's faith was not broken. By adopting the persona of God, he ensured that Lencho would believe the money came directly from the divine, thereby preserving the sanctity of his belief.

Q30: The story has been interpreted as a subtle critique of organized religion. Do you agree?

A: While not a harsh critique, one interpretation suggests that Lencho's belief is so literal and rigid that he misses the real-world manifestation of charity. The story critiques a faith that is so focused on the divine that it fails to recognize and appreciate the goodness and humanity of others, which is often a problem in literal or dogmatic interpretations of faith.
