1. Foundations of Evolution

Key Definitions

  • Early Humans: Lived in Prehistoric Times (no reading/writing). Communication was via sign language or special drums.
  • Evolution: Development from simple early forms to more advanced ones over millennia.
  • Modern Theory: Humans and modern apes share a same ape-like ancestor (separated ~5 million years ago).

Sources of Information (No Written Records)

  • Anthropologists: Study bones and skeletons of early humans and animals.
  • Palaeontologists: Study Fossils (traces of plants/animals preserved in Earth's crust).
  • Artifacts: Evidence like Tools, Weapons, Shelters, and Cave Paintings.

3. The Stone Age Timeline

Historical Divisions:

Palaeolithic (Old Stone Age) Mesolithic (Middle Stone Age) Neolithic (New Stone Age) Chalcolithic (Stone-Copper Age)

Palaeolithic Age (Old Stone Age)

Lifestyle: Hunter-Gatherers & Nomads

  • Nomadic Life: Moved based on seasons and following animal migration routes (Hunter-Gatherers).

    Challenges: 1) Many animals ran faster. 2) Dangerous close contact led to injury/death.

  • Tools: Made by chipping stone. Discovered Flint stone (easily shaped). Tools included knives, spears, axes, and harpoons. During excavations, many of these stone tools still retain their sharpness.
  • Shelters: Lived in caves, under rock overhangs, on trees, or out in the open (reinforced with animal skins/leaves).
  • Art: Made vivid Cave Paintings on walls (for recreation or religious significance).
  • Landmark Discovery: The ability to start a fire on their own (discovered by chance/accident).
  • Fire Uses: Cooked food, warmth, light in dark caves, and scaring away wild animals.

Mesolithic Age (Middle Stone Age)

Key Advancements: Domestication & Agriculture

  • Lifestyle Shift: Continued hunting/gathering, but began to domesticate animals.
  • First Domesticated Animal: The Wolf.
  • Economy: Saw the beginning of agriculture (e.g., cultivation of bamboo and sugarcane).
  • Tools: Began to make finer and smaller tools (microliths) like improved arrowheads, spearheads, canoes, and bows for hunting.
  • Art: Enjoyed painting (depicting birds, animals, and humans) and created small sculptures from stone, clay, and bone.

4. Quick Revision Q&A

Q1: What is the main source of information about early humans?

A: Since there are no written records, information comes from Anthropologists (bones/skeletons), Palaeontologists (fossils), and Artifacts (tools, weapons, paintings).

Q2: What is the key difference between Homo Erectus and Homo Sapiens?

A: Homo Erectus were the Upright Walkers and first to use fire, while Homo Sapiens are the "Thinking People" (modern humans).

Q3: Which Stone Age period marked the beginning of agriculture and animal domestication?

A: The Mesolithic Age (Middle Stone Age).

Q4: What was the main challenge faced by the Palaeolithic hunter-gatherers?

A: Animals often ran faster than them, making hunting difficult, and close contact led to danger, injury, or death.
